Your first lawn can feel overwhelming—there’s mowing, edging, watering, weeds, and brown spots, and it’s easy to wonder where to begin. The good news: you don’t have to do everything at once. Start with the **basics** that make the biggest difference, then add more as you get comfortable.

## Why the lawn matters

A neat lawn improves [curb appeal and how your home is perceived](/blog/why-a-great-lawn-matters)—by you, your neighbors, and future buyers. You don’t need a golf course; you need **consistent care**: regular mowing, clean edges, and enough water when it’s dry.

## Priority 1: Mow regularly

**Set a height** (about 3–3½ inches for most turf in Central Illinois) and **mow often enough** that you never remove more than about one-third of the grass blade. That keeps the lawn healthy and even. For the full how-to, see [how to mow like a pro](/blog/how-to-mow-like-a-pro).

If you don’t own a mower yet, you can hire the job out. Many new homeowners use a service for the first season while they learn the property and decide whether they want to DIY long-term.

## Priority 2: Edge and trim

Clean lines along the driveway and walkway, plus trimming around fences and beds, make the whole yard look **finished**. Do it every time you mow (or every other time) so it never gets out of hand. Details in [edging and trimming basics](/blog/how-to-edging-trimming).

## Priority 3: Water when it’s dry

Lawns in our area typically need about **1–1½ inches of water per week** from rain and/or irrigation. Water deeply and less often (early morning is best). For more, see [watering your lawn](/blog/watering-your-lawn).

## What to add next

Once mowing, edging, and watering are under control, you can layer in:

- **Fertilizing** — [fertilizing 101](/blog/fertilizing-101) and our [seasonal lawn care calendar](/blog/seasonal-lawn-care-calendar).
- **Weeds** — [dealing with weeds](/blog/dealing-with-weeds).
- **Brown or thin spots** — [brown spots and dog spots](/blog/brown-spots-dog-spots).

Take it one season at a time. You’ll learn what your lawn needs and how much you want to do yourself.
